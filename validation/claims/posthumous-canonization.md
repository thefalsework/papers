# `posthumous-canonization` — Posthumous / delayed canonization as a testable prediction

**Status:** OPEN
**Paper:** Paper 6 § 5, § 7 (v2.0, exploratory companion)
**Domain:** History of art / history of music / historical musicology / reception studies; empirical humanities
**Time estimate:** variable (see below)

---

## Background

Paper 6 (Canonical Confrontation) proposes that canonical status is the structural trace of direct practitioner confrontation with a domain's kernel at the point where the comma is operative. The account is exploratory and is explicitly not a derivation from Papers 1–5 — it applies the kernel/comma topology of Paper 1 to the question of why certain works sustain canonical status across centuries.

From § 5 of Paper 6:

> *Canonical works persist because the comma they confront stays open. The reason people keep returning to Bach, to Cézanne, to Coltrane is not tradition or institutional inertia. It is that the structural gap these works engage has not closed and cannot close.*

And from § 7 (open questions):

> **Posthumous and delayed canonisation.** The structural account predicts that works engaging the kernel at its limit will eventually achieve canonical recognition regardless of initial reception, because the structural trace they carry remains detectable. Bach's eighty-year neglect and subsequent return, El Greco's three-century rehabilitation, and similar cases are not anomalies but expected consequences. This prediction is testable against the historical record.

## The claim

Paper 6 commits to one empirically testable sub-claim:

> If canonical status is the structural trace of kernel-level confrontation, then posthumous / delayed canonical rehabilitation should correlate with recognition — later in the reception history — that the work confronted the domain's generative operation at its limit. Conversely, the falsification condition is: identification of works that exhibit the structural markers of kernel-level confrontation (comparable to Bach, El Greco, or other rehabilitated canonical cases) but that have not been recovered by canonical reception despite sufficient elapsed time.

The claim is about the relationship between **structural markers** (discernible in the work itself and in the reception discourse that eventually recovers it) and **canonical persistence / rehabilitation** (discernible in the historical record).

## What's being asked

1. **Is the historical record as described?** Does the reception history of the paradigm cases the paper cites — Bach (≈ 80-year neglect, Mendelssohn-era rehabilitation), El Greco (≈ 3-century neglect, early-20th-century rehabilitation), the Homeric epics (predating every institution that has subsequently claimed them) — fit the pattern the paper claims? Or are these cases atypical / cherry-picked?
2. **Are there matched cases of long-neglected works that exhibit comparable structural markers but have *not* achieved canonical rehabilitation?** Such cases, if numerous, would falsify the prediction or at minimum require the paper to refine what it means by "sufficient elapsed time."
3. **Is the class of "structural markers" operationalizable independently of reception history?** Paper 6 ties the operationalization to the proxy feature program of Paper 1 § 4.4; historians / musicologists / art historians may have independent vocabularies for what the paper calls "kernel-level confrontation" (e.g., contrapuntal density in Bach scholarship, formal-material tension in El Greco literature). A partial translation between vocabularies is part of what validation here would produce.
4. **Is the account competitive with sociological / institutional accounts of canonical rehabilitation?** If the sociological account is sufficient, what additional explanatory work does the structural account do? If it is not sufficient, where does the structural account outperform it?

## What a resolution would look like

A resolution is unlikely to be a single one-hour check. Plausible resolutions include:

- **Systematic historical test.** A historian of music, a historian of art, or a literary historian identifies ≥ 10 cases of delayed / posthumous canonical rehabilitation in their domain, characterizes the markers that rehabilitation cited, and reports whether those markers are structural (in Paper 6's sense) or primarily sociological / institutional. If consistently structural, this strengthens the prediction; if consistently sociological, it weakens it.
- **Counter-example census.** A scholar identifies a class of works that exhibit strong structural markers but have not been canonically rehabilitated despite long elapsed time. The paper would need to either refine the prediction (e.g., elapsed-time threshold) or retract it.
- **Domain-specific refinement.** The prediction may hold in some domains (music, painting) and fail in others (architecture, literature). A validator who finds the pattern asymmetric across domains would thereby refine Paper 6 § 7's cross-domain-invariance open question.
- **Rejection.** The reception history of the paradigm cases does not fit the pattern the paper claims, even on sympathetic reading, and § 5 / § 7 of Paper 6 should be rewritten.

## Why this claim is distinctive

Paper 6 is exploratory and most of its content is structural-interpretive rather than testable. The posthumous-canonization prediction is the single sub-claim in the paper that is *specifically* empirically testable against a defined historical record. Anchoring validation effort on this sub-claim is therefore the most productive use of a historian's or reception-studies scholar's time.

This claim is also the natural bridge between Paper 6's exploratory register and the more formal validation items carried by Papers 1, 3, and 5: if the posthumous-canonization prediction holds up empirically, it gives the kernel/comma topology empirical traction beyond its use as a structural vocabulary; if it fails, Paper 6's register remains exploratory but its scope narrows accordingly.
