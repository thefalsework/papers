# `ellis-cartwright-philosophical-support` — Ellis and Cartwright as scholarly precedent for domain-dependent formalism

**Status:** OPEN
**Papers:** Paper 1 § 5.4 (v11.4); Paper 3 § 7.3 (v9.2)
**Domain(s):** Philosophy of science (emergence, multi-level ontology, philosophy of physics, philosophy of mathematics)
**Time estimate for a competent validator:** ~2–4 hours (two primary texts plus the adjacent paper sections)
**GitHub issue:** [#10](https://github.com/thefalsework/papers/issues/10)

## Background

The FalseWork papers adopt, across their cumulative argument, a specific methodological commitment: *different domains admit different degrees of formalism.* Papers 3 § 5 and 5 handle number theory and set theory where full formal derivation is possible (Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic; Cantor 1891; Baker 1966). Paper 3 § 4 handles category-theoretic formalization of the music kernel where formalism is possible but specialist-contested ([`music-kernel-umbrella.md`](music-kernel-umbrella.md)). Papers 1 and 3 § 7 mark the G∧R∧C extension to practice domains (music, cinema, architecture, literature) explicitly as *structural analogy* rather than formal derivation — a downgrade the papers adopt at v11.3 (Paper 1) and v9.0 (Paper 3), tracked at [`g-r-c-practice-domains.md`](g-r-c-practice-domains.md).

This claim asks whether that methodological commitment has scholarly standing in contemporary philosophy of science independent of the FalseWork programme. The specific candidate precedents are:

1. **George F. R. Ellis's multi-level emergence framework.** Primary: Ellis, G.F.R. (2016). *How Can Physics Underlie the Mind? Top-Down Causation in the Human Context.* Springer. Secondary: Ellis & Drossel (2019, 2020, various) on contextual emergence; Ellis (2005), "Physics, complexity and causality," *Nature* 435: 743; Ellis (2006), "Issues in the Philosophy of Cosmology," in *Handbook of the Philosophy of Physics*. Ellis argues that different organizational levels (physics, chemistry, biology, psychology, social) require different formalisms and that higher-level descriptions are not reducible to lower-level physics. "Top-down causation" — the causal efficacy of higher-level structure on lower-level dynamics — is argued as a specific technical position.

2. **Nancy Cartwright's dappled-world thesis.** Primary: Cartwright, N. (1999). *The Dappled World: A Study of the Boundaries of Science.* Cambridge University Press. Cartwright argues that scientific laws are local and domain-specific, that the universe is a "dappled" patchwork of different kinds of order, and that the single-formalism ideal is a methodological error.

Both are established, widely-cited positions in contemporary philosophy of science:

- Ellis is a relativistic cosmologist (co-author with S. W. Hawking of *The Large Scale Structure of Space-Time*, 1973), University of Cape Town emeritus, Templeton Prize recipient.
- Cartwright is Emeritus Professor at the London School of Economics and Research Professor at UC San Diego, Fellow of the British Academy.

Both argue, on independent grounds within mainstream philosophy of science, that domain-dependent formalism is a principled stance.

## The claim for validation

*Ellis's multi-level emergence framework (Ellis 2016; Ellis & Drossel 2019, 2020) and Cartwright's dappled-world thesis (Cartwright 1999) provide independent scholarly precedent for the FalseWork programme's methodological commitment that different domains admit different degrees of formalism, and that structural-analogical reasoning at appropriate registers is a legitimate rather than defective methodological posture.*

The claim is explicitly one of **adjacent scholarly support, not endorsement.** Neither Ellis nor Cartwright has engaged with the FalseWork programme, the G∧R∧C condition, the kernel/comma topology, or the five-position derivation. The question for validation is narrower: *are Ellis 2016 and Cartwright 1999 correctly scoped as adjacent scholarly precedent for the specific methodological commitment, or is that framing overstated?*

## Named disjoints

Three specific ways the mapping might be wrong, each of which a validator should assess:

1. **Vertical vs. horizontal hierarchy.** Ellis's emergence is vertical: physics → chemistry → biology → psychology → social. FalseWork's domains are horizontal: music, cinema, architecture, literature, software, physics. Ellis's framework may sanction multi-level vertical emergence without sanctioning cross-horizontal-domain analogy. A validator should assess whether Ellis's argument extends to horizontal domain comparison or is strictly vertical.

2. **Primary object difference.** Ellis focuses on causation (top-down, contextual). FalseWork focuses on kernel/comma topology. Cartwright focuses on laws and capacities. The shared commitment (domain-dependent formalism) may not entail a shared theoretical framework. A validator should assess whether the shared meta-methodological posture carries more than meta-methodological support.

3. **Cartwright's naturalism vs. FalseWork's structural claim.** Cartwright's dappled world is explicitly empirical and anti-realist about unified laws. FalseWork's five-position derivation claims a mutually-exclusive-collectively-exhaustive structure across domains — an arguably universal structural claim. These commitments may be in tension, not alignment. A validator should assess whether Cartwright's thesis is genuinely supportive of FalseWork's specific claims or only of the anti-reductionist meta-posture.

## What a validation response should cover

For each of Ellis and Cartwright, a validator should state:

1. **Confirmed as scoped** — citation is appropriately scoped as adjacent scholarly precedent for domain-dependent formalism; no stronger claim is implicit; the disjoints named above are accurately described.
2. **Confirmed with amendment** — citation is appropriate but the scoping requires refinement. Propose the specific refinement.
3. **Requires restricted use** — citation is misleading as currently framed. Propose either a narrower use or removal.
4. **Incorrect / absent standing** — framework does not apply. Propose an alternative scholarly precedent or acknowledge that the papers should hedge without citing Ellis/Cartwright.

A validation confirming one and restricting the other is a useful response. Partial validations are welcomed.

## Alternative precedents worth considering

A validator may also propose scholars whose frameworks fit more cleanly than Ellis or Cartwright. Candidates worth evaluating:

- **Sandra Mitchell** (*Unsimple Truths: Science, Complexity, and Policy*, 2009; *Biological Complexity and Integrative Pluralism*, 2003) — integrative pluralism; likely the closest living fit for FalseWork's horizontal domain pluralism.
- **Philip Clayton** (*Mind and Emergence: From Quantum to Consciousness*, 2004) — emergence across scales; overlaps substantially with Ellis.
- **Jerry Fodor** ("Special Sciences, or the Disunity of Science as a Working Hypothesis," *Synthese*, 1974) — the classic anti-reductionist case for domain-specific formalism in the special sciences.
- **Paul Humphreys** (*Emergence: A Philosophical Account*, 2016) — emergence and computational methods.
- **Ian Hacking** ("Language, Truth and Reason," in *Rationality and Relativism*, 1982; subsequent work on "styles of reasoning") — different domains admit different formalism-styles.

If any of these provides a cleaner precedent than the current Ellis/Cartwright pairing, the papers should be updated accordingly.

## Caveats

- This is a philosophy-of-science claim about methodological posture, not a claim about whether FalseWork's specific technical results (G∧R∧C derivation, five-position typology, kernel/comma topology) are correct. Those are tracked in separate claim files.
- The claim is load-bearing in a specific narrow way: if the Ellis/Cartwright scholarship does not support domain-dependent formalism as a principled stance, the relevant paragraphs in Papers 1 and 3 either need re-grounding or removal. The rest of the papers' arguments are not affected.
- The FalseWork programme cites Ellis 2016 and Cartwright 1999 without either scholar's knowledge or consent. Both scholars are living (as of 2026-04-21) and could be contacted directly if a validator judges that appropriate.
